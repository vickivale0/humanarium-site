Are Human Journalists Truly Irreplaceable?

A new essay from the Reuters Institute argues the real threat to journalism isn’t automation — it’s a value system that lets speed and scale quietly outrank human judgment.

By Çiğdem Öztabak · Founder Summary · Reuters Institute · May 19, 2026 3 min read

Much of the media industry, a recent Reuters Institute essay argues, is imaginatively stuck on AI — fixated on defending journalists’ place rather than rethinking what actually serves the public. Its author, Agnes Stenbom Swedling, flips the anxious question: the point isn’t whether humans are irreplaceable, but which human contributions are genuinely unique, and worth protecting.

“Responsible AI” has quietly come to mean keeping a human in the loop. But oversight doesn’t scale with complexity, and it can reduce people to what one scholar calls moral crumple zones — the humans who absorb the blame when a machine gets it wrong. Framing our value as accountability-bearers, she warns, leaves most of what we can actually do unused.

Her alternative is hybridisation — humans and machines making journalism together — and she draws a sharp line through it. In the machine-centric version, AI automates and scales while people hover at the edges. In the human-centric version, machines handle the baseline while humans do the parts that resist digitisation: deciding what matters, interpreting significance, sitting across from another person and asking the harder question.

The trap is that most newsrooms believe they’re building the second and are quietly building the first — optimising for what machines do well and reshaping human work to fit. Once that inversion is baked into the infrastructure, it becomes very hard to reverse.

The danger is not automation itself, but a value system where speed outranks human judgment.

Journalism, she reminds us, was never just a workflow. It holds judgments — what is worth noticing, what is fair, what is true in context and not merely in data. The capacities we still call uniquely human — empathy, presence, judgment, creativity, vision — may be the whole point.

Why this matters to us

This is the same question we keep circling at Humanarium — in newsrooms and in life. When we let the machine’s logic reorganise us, the first things to go are the human ones: presence, judgment, the room. The answer isn’t to opt out. It’s to design, on purpose, for what only people can do.
