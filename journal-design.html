On Design

A Club for Humans Should Not Look Like a Machine

Notes on designing Humanarium — paper, ink, and the colour of oxytocin.

By Çiğdem Öztabak New York · 2026 4 min read

Most things built in the machine age look like the machine age. Cool greys, a sans-serif borrowed from a thousand other dashboards, a gradient, a great deal of white space that reads less like calm than like a server room. The aesthetic of optimisation. I understand why — it signals competence, speed, the future. But when we began designing Humanarium, a club for humans, I knew the one thing it could not look like was a machine.

Design is not decoration. It is an argument you make before anyone has read a word. By the time a visitor reaches our first sentence, the page has already told them what we believe — about attention, about warmth, about whether the person on the other end matters. So the form had to carry the belief. If we are building a place to protect human connection, the surface itself has to feel human: made, warm, a little imperfect, the way a hand is.

Design is an argument you make before anyone has read a word.

We started with paper. Not pure white — pure white is clinical, the white of forms and waiting rooms — but a warm, soft white, the colour of good stock under a reading lamp. Over everything we laid the faintest grain, the tooth of a page that has been printed rather than rendered. Our reference was never another website. It was the poster in the window of a downtown bookshop; the programme handed to you at a small theatre — things that assume you can read, that you have time, that you came to be among people. I have spent years with medieval manuscripts, and what stays with me is never the information. It is the evidence of the hand — the warmth of someone having been there, on the page, for you.

Then, two voices. For the words that carry feeling, a warm literary serif with a little idiosyncrasy in its bones. For the words that carry fact — the dates, the numbers, the labels — a precise monospace, the typeface of the lab and the field note. I wanted the tension between them: a humanist who keeps a research ledger; a research institute that has not forgotten it is run by people. That pairing is the whole personality of the brand, in two fonts.

And one colour. This was the decision I cared about most. The accent of a club built on human connection should, I thought, be oxytocin — the hormone of touch and trust, the warmth you feel when someone you love walks into the room. There is only one problem: oxytocin is colourless. It is a small, clear peptide; it has no colour at all. I did not want to pretend otherwise, because the whole point of this place is to be honest — about machines, and about ourselves. So we did not colour the molecule. We coloured the feeling. We gave the warmth a colour — a soft, almost neon orange — and we let it behave like warmth: used rarely, never filling the room, always glowing faintly, breathing in and out behind the words like body heat, like a pulse.

We did not colour the molecule. We coloured the feeling.

Most of the design is restraint, and restraint, done right, reads as warmth. One accent. Wide margins. Hairline rules instead of boxes and shadows. Saying less, and trusting the reader to lean in — which is, after all, the behaviour we are trying to bring back. There is exactly one dark moment on the whole site, where the orange is finally allowed to glow against near-black, like a fire you gather around. Scarcity is what gives it heat.

Even the portraits make the argument. Our faces sit in a warm black-and-white, and bloom into full colour the moment you reach toward them — a hover, the closest thing the web has to touch. Connection, made visible. Reach, and the person warms.

None of this is style for its own sake. I have a quiet allergy to design that exists only to look designed. Every choice here is trying to do one small thing: to make you feel, before you have decided to feel anything, that you have walked into a room you would not want to leave — because there are other people in it. In an age that is teaching us, gently and constantly, to be alone together with our machines, that felt like the most important argument we could make. And the first place to make it was here, on the page — in paper, in ink, and in the colour of a hug.
